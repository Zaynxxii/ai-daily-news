2

Open Source Licensing in the AI Era

The chardet controversy exposes gray areas in open-source licensing for the AI age. LGPL requires derivative works to maintain the same license, but does a 'ground-up rewrite' count as derivative? If AI reads LGPL code and generates MIT-licensed 'inspirationally similar' code, is the original license retroactive? This dispute may establish precedent: do maintenance contributions grant license change rights? Does 'spiritual ownership' have an expiration date?

3

AI-Native Attack Surfaces in CI/CD

Clinejection demonstrates an unprecedented attack chain: prompt injection → Bash command execution → cache poisoning → key theft → supply chain poisoning. Traditional CI/CD security assumes 'workflow definitions are trusted,' but AI agents convert natural language input (issue titles) directly to command execution. This is the first real-world demonstration of AI-native attacks. Defense requires: (1) physical isolation of AI workflows from release workflows, (2) namespace-isolated caches per workflow, (3) prompt input sanitization.

4

iOS Exploit Weaponization Democratization

Google TAG's Coruna report reveals a disturbing reality: 23 iOS exploits, 5 complete chains, proliferating from surveillance vendors → Russian espionage → Chinese financial crime groups, forming a complete 'second-hand exploit' market. Exploit weaponization is democratizing — no nation-state capabilities required, just buy an exploit kit to attack all iOS 13-17.2.1 devices. This shows zero-day exploits' 'half-life' is shortening — once deployed, they quickly enter the black market.

5

RSS as Weapon Against Platform Control

Cory Doctorow revisits the history of Google Reader's death: In 2013, Google deliberately killed the most popular RSS reader to force G+ adoption, forcing users to discover content through G+. This is a classic case of 'platform enclosure' — replacing open protocols with closed ecosystems. Doctorow notes 'enshittification' isn't economic inevitability but policy choice. The RSS revival (and recent Reader Mode comeback) represents users regaining control of information access. In the AI summary era, RSS value is more prominent — it's the raw information source unfiltered by algorithms.

6

The 'Lone Wolf' Developer in the LLM Era

Jeff Geerling observes a phenomenon: LLMs let senior developers independently complete work that originally required team collaboration. 'Expert Beginners' and 'Lone Wolves' will dominate the early LLM era. This is a double-edged sword: on one hand, individual productivity greatly improves; on the other, code review, knowledge sharing, and collective wisdom may weaken. When one person can replace a team, should we celebrate efficiency gains or worry about technical debt accumulation?
